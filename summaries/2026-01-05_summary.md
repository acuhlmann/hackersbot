### 3. Why does a least squares fit appear to have a bias when applied to simple data?

**URL:** [https://stats.stackexchange.com/questions/674129/why-does-a-linear-least-squares-fit-appear-to-have-a-bias-when-applied-to-simple](https://stats.stackexchange.com/questions/674129/why-does-a-linear-least-squares-fit-appear-to-have-a-bias-when-applied-to-simple)
**Points:** 85 | **Author:** azeemba | **Time:** 2026-01-04T20:25:30 1767558330 | **Comments:** 20

#### Article Summary

The text discusses why a standard linear least-squares fit can appear biased when applied to correlated data, as it minimizes vertical errors rather than aligning with the data's main spread. The author compares this to the principal eigenvector from PCA, which points in the direction of maximum variance and better captures the data's central orientation. This difference arises because least squares assumes one variable is independent, breaking symmetry, while PCA treats both symmetrically. The example uses generated correlated data to illustrate the contrast.

#### Overall Comment Discussion Summary

This discussion clarifies that ordinary least squares (OLS) regression assumes noise only in the Y variable, while methods like total least squares or PCA account for noise in both X and Y. Contributors note that Deming regression is a preferred alternative when X has error, and explain that OLS minimizes vertical distances, leading to a different slope than modeling horizontal distances. Several comments emphasize that OLS remains unbiased for predicting Y given X even if X is noisy, and its validity relies on error properties like constant variance, not strictly a Gaussian distribution. Visual discrepancies in fits are expected and do not necessarily indicate a poor model.
